\begin{abstract}
Shared-autonomy systems often decide who should control the next action from intent confidence, elapsed time, or a scalar arbitration score. These signals are useful, but they hide the physical condition that matters for safe transfer: authority should move only when the current interaction state crosses a boundary where human control, robot control, or shared control becomes admissible. A previous 600-case diagnostic showed that hand-coded authority boundaries exposed this issue, but a tuned phase+quality/risk threshold recovered the old labels perfectly, proving that the original benchmark was too easy. We therefore study a stronger setting: authority boundaries under cross-user, cross-robot, contact, sensing, and distribution shift. We introduce a \emph{control authority boundary field}, a compositional state representation that combines task phase, user input quality, contact risk, force margin, latency, uncertainty, and hysteresis before ceding or reclaiming authority. The full-scale deterministic benchmark spans 12 authority-transfer scenarios, 5 robot platform families, 6 user regimes, 6 contact/risk regimes, 5 sensing regimes, 4 shift regimes, and 7 policies, producing 302,400 compact condition rows that represent 41.37 billion trajectory evaluations and 3.31 trillion authority-frame decisions. The boundary field reaches 0.891 safe task success, 0.020 unsafe ceding, 0.022 missed reclaim, 0.018 chatter, 0.711 boundary F1, and 0.680 utility. The strongest tuned phase+quality/risk threshold reaches 0.852 success, 0.041 unsafe ceding, 0.039 missed reclaim, 0.110 chatter, 0.717 F1, and 0.578 utility. The result reframes authority transfer as a shift-robust physical boundary problem rather than an intent-confidence threshold.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Shared autonomy has a charmingly simple promise: the human contributes intent and judgment, the robot contributes precision and safety, and an arbitration policy decides how much authority each side should have. In practice, the hard part is not blending two commands. The hard part is knowing when authority is physically admissible. A human may clearly intend to move forward, but the robot may be in a contact-insertion phase where ceding authority would cause a force spike. A robot may have a correct plan, but in a handover or inspection phase over-control can be intrusive, slow, or socially inappropriate. The relevant question is not only ``who is confident?'' It is ``where is the coupled human-robot system relative to a boundary where authority transfer changes safety and task value?''

This paper develops that question into a state representation. We call it a \emph{control authority boundary field}. The field is not a single threshold on confidence or risk. It is a compositional boundary over task phase, user input quality, robot contact state, force margin, latency, uncertainty, and authority hysteresis. It tells the robot when ceding authority is physically admissible, when reclaiming authority is necessary, and when shared control should persist to avoid chatter.

The repository originally contained a 600-case synthetic diagnostic. That diagnostic was useful because it showed that confidence-only and timer-only arbitration could cede authority unsafely in contact phases. However, v2 hardening added tuned threshold baselines. A tuned phase+quality/risk rule achieved perfect holdout success and zero unsafe ceding on the old split. That result narrowed the claim. It showed that the old labels could be recovered by a simple threshold rule and therefore did not establish a novel switching algorithm.

Rather than hide that result, we use it as the starting point. The right next question is not whether a fixed hand-coded rule beats a tuned threshold on one small in-distribution diagnostic. The right question is whether authority-transfer policies remain safe and useful when the boundary moves. In real shared autonomy, boundaries move across users, robots, contact dynamics, sensing delay, and task phase. A threshold tuned on one diagnostic can be correct in-distribution and brittle under shift.

We therefore evaluate authority policies in a much larger deterministic full-scale suite. The suite spans twelve scenarios from free-space alignment to constrained peg-in-hole, five robot families from assistive arms to exoskeletons, six user regimes including overconfidence and tremor, six risk regimes, five sensing/control regimes, four shift regimes, and seven policies. Each compact condition row represents many user seeds, trajectory variants, payload variants, supervisor noise variants, trials, and control frames.

The result is a strong but bounded positive finding. The oracle remains best. The tuned phase+quality/risk threshold remains a strong baseline and slightly leads raw boundary F1. But the boundary field is the best non-oracle policy in utility, safe task success, unsafe ceding, missed reclaim, authority chatter, peak force, and recovery latency. The field gains because it treats authority transfer as a physical boundary problem with hysteresis and uncertainty, not as a static score.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper makes four contributions.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item It reframes shared-autonomy authority transfer as inference over a compositional physical boundary field rather than an intent-confidence or timer threshold.
\item It uses the old tuned-threshold success as a negative control, showing why in-distribution label recovery is insufficient evidence for authority-transfer robustness.
\item It introduces a 302,400-row deterministic benchmark representing 41.37 billion trajectory evaluations and 3.31 trillion authority-frame decisions across users, robots, tasks, risk regimes, sensing regimes, and shift regimes.
\item It shows that the control authority boundary field is the best non-oracle policy under the full shifted suite, with 0.891 success, 0.020 unsafe ceding, 0.022 missed reclaim, 0.018 chatter, and 0.680 utility.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Authority Transfer as a Boundary Problem}

Let $\state_t$ denote the shared-autonomy interaction state. It includes task phase $\phase_t$, human input quality $\quality_t$, intent confidence, force or contact risk $\risk_t$, physical margin, sensing latency $\latency_t$, uncertainty $\uncert_t$, and the current authority mode. A conventional scalar arbitrator computes a blend parameter $\alpha_t$:
\[
u_t = \alpha_t u_h + (1-\alpha_t) u_r,
\]
where $u_h$ is the human command and $u_r$ is the robot command. The blend parameter is often monotone in intent confidence, robot confidence, or elapsed time.

A boundary view asks a different question. It asks whether a change in authority is physically admissible:
\[
\field(\state_t) =
  f(\phase_t,\quality_t,\risk_t,m_t,\latency_t,\uncert_t,a_{t-1}),
\]
where $m_t$ is physical margin and $a_{t-1}$ is the previous authority mode. The field outputs whether ceding, reclaiming, or maintaining shared control is appropriate. It is a field because the boundary varies across the interaction state space; it is not a global scalar threshold.

\subsection{Ceding and Reclaiming Are Different}

Ceding authority and reclaiming authority are not symmetric. Ceding asks whether the human should receive more direct control. Reclaiming asks whether robot assistance or protection should interrupt or constrain human control. A policy can be too conservative and intervene unnecessarily, or too permissive and cede authority unsafely. It can also chatter, rapidly switching modes near a boundary. These failure modes are different and need separate metrics.

The boundary field therefore evaluates three decisions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item Is human authority admissible in the current phase?
\item Is robot reclaim required by risk, margin, latency, or uncertainty?
\item Is the state near enough to the boundary that hysteresis should preserve the current mode?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why Intent Confidence Is Insufficient}

Intent confidence estimates whether the system understands what the human wants. That is useful, but it does not prove that the desired action is physically safe. An overconfident operator may express clear intent during a force-limited insertion. A delayed intent estimator may be confident about the previous phase. A tremor/noisy input regime may make confidence oscillate near a boundary. Physical authority transfer must therefore condition on more than intent.

\subsection{Why Tuned Thresholds Are Not Enough}

Tuned thresholds are strong baselines. If the environment is stable, the task phases are fixed, and labels are generated by threshold-like rules, a threshold can recover labels perfectly. The v2 diagnostic showed exactly that. The weakness appears when the threshold is moved out of its calibration distribution. User quality shifts, force calibration shifts, contact dynamics shifts, and asynchronous sensing can all change where authority should transfer. A boundary field represents this explicitly by composing the variables that move the boundary.

\section{Relation to Prior Work}

Shared control and shared autonomy have long studied blending, assistance, and intent inference. Dragan and Srinivasa formalized policy blending for shared control \citep{dragan2013}. Javdani et al. framed shared autonomy through hindsight optimization \citep{javdani2015}. Reddy et al. studied shared autonomy through deep reinforcement learning \citep{reddy2018}. Losey et al. and related work investigate how robots should adapt to partners and roles in collaborative settings \citep{losey2018}.

The present paper does not claim that adaptive authority allocation is new. The novelty is the boundary framing and the stress test. Instead of asking whether an arbitration score can fit a label split, we ask whether the physical variables that authorize ceding and reclaiming remain explicit under shift. The old v2 threshold result is exactly the warning: a simple threshold can solve a narrow diagnostic without providing a robust authority model.

\subsection{Scenarios}

The 12 scenarios cover free-space, contact, human interaction, and recovery. Free-space alignment and remote inspection are high-cede regimes where human authority is often appropriate. Contact insertion, slip recovery, mobile-base docking, and constrained peg-in-hole are low-cede regimes where risk and force margin dominate. Handover, shared guidance, occluded handoff, cooperative transport, and tool exchange are mixed regimes where authority depends strongly on user quality, sensing, and phase.

\subsection{Robot Platforms}

The five platforms are assistive arm, mobile manipulator, surgical teleoperation arm, warehouse cobot, and powered exoskeleton. They differ in autonomy, force control, precision, latency, and intrusion cost. A surgical teleoperation arm has high precision and high intrusion cost. A mobile manipulator has broad dynamics and higher latency. An exoskeleton makes unnecessary intervention especially costly because authority affects the user's body directly.

\subsection{User and Risk Regimes}

User regimes include expert steady, novice exploratory, fatigued operator, tremor/noisy input, delayed intent expression, and overconfident operator. Risk regimes include open workspace, clutter proximity, human proximity, fragile object, force-limited insertion, and payload instability. The cross-product matters because authority transfer is not just about the user or the environment alone. Overconfidence is more dangerous near humans and fragile objects than in open workspace; tremor matters more during insertion than remote inspection.

\subsection{Sensing and Shift}

Sensing regimes include clean low latency, delayed command channel, noisy force estimate, intermittent intent estimate, and asynchronous robot/user state. Shift regimes include in-distribution, user calibration shift, contact dynamics shift, and compounded user/contact/sensing shift. The shift regimes are the central v3 addition. They test whether a policy that looks strong on one calibration remains safe and useful when the boundary changes.

\section{Policies}

The benchmark compares seven policies.

\paragraph{Confidence-only switch.}
This policy cedes authority when intent confidence is high. It is intentionally simple and captures the common failure where clear intent is mistaken for safe control.

\paragraph{Timer-decay switch.}
This policy changes authority with elapsed interaction progress and latency. It can reduce some over-control but cannot explain why a phase is physically safe or unsafe.

\paragraph{Tuned quality/risk threshold.}
This baseline thresholds user input quality and estimated risk. It is a stronger physical baseline than confidence or timer rules.

\paragraph{Tuned phase+quality/risk threshold.}
This is the strongest v2 baseline. It uses phase membership plus quality and risk thresholds. It remains strong in the full benchmark and is the main non-oracle comparator.

\paragraph{Scalar risk-score arbitration.}
This policy uses a weighted scalar score over confidence, quality, phase, margin, risk, and latency. It is more expressive than a fixed threshold but still lacks hysteretic boundary structure.

\paragraph{Control authority boundary field.}
The proposed policy composes physical cede admissibility, phase, input quality, adaptive margin, contact boundary, intent, user adaptation, uncertainty, calibration shift, and safety pressure. It includes hysteresis-like suppression of chatter and an irreducible learned-policy risk floor.

\paragraph{Oracle boundary supervisor.}
The oracle has privileged boundary supervision and low residual risk. It should remain best overall.

\section{Metrics}

We report safe task success, unsafe cede rate, missed reclaim rate, needless intervention rate, authority chatter rate, mean peak force, recovery latency, boundary F1, and utility. Unsafe ceding measures giving the human authority when the physical boundary requires robot protection. Missed reclaim measures failure to retake or preserve robot authority when risk rises. Needless intervention measures retaining or reclaiming authority when human control is admissible. Chatter measures unstable mode switching near a boundary.

Utility combines these metrics with strong penalties for unsafe ceding, missed reclaim, chatter, and needless intervention. The utility is a benchmark score rather than a universal shared-autonomy objective. It is designed to make safety and stability matter more than tiny changes in success.

\section{Implementation}

The full-scale runner is \texttt{scripts/run\_full\_scale\_authority\_boundary\_suite.py}. It streams compact condition rows to \texttt{results/full\_scale/condition\_metrics.csv} and accumulates policy, shift, scenario, robot, and user summaries. The runner also writes generated LaTeX tables and PDF figures.

The row model computes observed input quality, intent confidence, latency, physical margin, hazard, uncertainty, phase cede tendency, cede truth, and safety pressure. Each policy converts those features into a cede decision. Metrics are deterministic expected outcomes under the represented seeds, trajectories, payload variants, trials, and frames.

This design keeps memory use low. The condition CSV is large enough to audit interactions but the runner never materializes all represented authority-frame decisions.

\section{Limitations}

The benchmark is deterministic and simulated. It does not prove safety on hardware. A real system would need calibrated force sensing, user studies, task-specific authority interfaces, physical contact validation, and out-of-distribution monitoring. The field should be interpreted as mechanism evidence and benchmark evidence, not as a deployment-ready safety certificate.

The policy equations are transparent and hand-designed. A learned boundary field could improve them, but it would also require careful generalization tests. The point of this paper is not that these exact coefficients should be deployed. The point is that authority transfer should expose the variables that move the boundary and should be evaluated under shifts that make static thresholds brittle.

\section{Conclusion}

Control authority in shared autonomy is not only a confidence score. It is a physical boundary condition. The v2 tuned-threshold result showed that narrow diagnostics can be solved by simple thresholds, so this paper moved to a larger shift and composition benchmark. In that benchmark, the control authority boundary field is the best non-oracle policy by utility, unsafe ceding, missed reclaim, chatter, and success. The result supports a practical design rule: before ceding or reclaiming authority, a robot should ask which physical boundary has been crossed and how stable that boundary is under user, robot, contact, sensing, and shift conditions.

\section{Factor Details}

The benchmark factors were chosen to make authority transfer move for physical reasons. Free-space alignment and remote inspection are high-cede scenarios because human control is often useful and risk is low. Contact insertion, slip recovery, mobile-base docking, and constrained peg-in-hole are low-cede scenarios because the robot must protect force margin and contact stability. Handover, shared guidance, occluded handoff, cooperative transport, and tool exchange are mixed because user intent, social appropriateness, and physical risk all matter.

Robot platforms cover different authority costs. An assistive arm should not surprise the user but has moderate force control. A mobile manipulator has larger dynamics and delay. A surgical teleoperation arm has high precision but high cost for unwanted intervention. A warehouse cobot often has strong autonomy but operates near clutter and payloads. A powered exoskeleton has unusually high intrusion cost because robot authority acts through the user's body.

User regimes are included because authority transfer should not assume a single operator model. Expert steady users can safely receive authority more often. Novices and fatigued operators may need more protection. Tremor/noisy input requires filtering and hysteresis. Delayed intent expression means intent confidence may lag phase. Overconfident operators create the classic failure of high intent with unsafe physical context.

\section{Metric Notes}

Safe task success measures whether the represented authority decision remains useful and safe. Unsafe cede measures the most dangerous failure: giving direct human authority when the physical state requires protection. Missed reclaim measures the related failure of not retaking authority when risk rises. Needless intervention measures over-control in states where ceding would be safe. Authority chatter measures instability near the boundary.

Boundary F1 is computed over reclaim-boundary detection. It is informative but not sufficient. A policy can increase recall by reclaiming too often, which raises needless intervention and chatter. The tuned phase threshold illustrates this tradeoff: it has slightly higher aggregate F1 than the boundary field, but it also has much higher chatter and worse utility. This is why the paper reports all metrics rather than choosing the metric most favorable to the proposed policy.

Peak force and recovery latency are proxy physical costs. They increase when the policy cedes unsafely, misses reclaim events, or chatters under latency and uncertainty. Utility combines success, unsafe ceding, missed reclaim, needless intervention, chatter, force, and recovery latency with heavier penalties on safety and stability failures.

\section{Why the V2 Result Matters}

The v2 threshold result is a useful guardrail. It prevents the paper from claiming too much from a small synthetic label split. The old data had six families and labels that could be recovered by phase, quality, and risk thresholds. A tuned threshold reaching perfect holdout performance shows that the old benchmark was too close to its own generating rule.

The v3 benchmark changes the question. It does not ask whether a threshold can fit labels sampled from one regime. It asks whether policies remain stable when the authority boundary moves. User calibration shift changes the meaning of input quality and confidence. Contact dynamics shift changes force and margin. Compounded shift changes user calibration, contact dynamics, latency, and noise at once. These shifts make boundary composition more important.

Keeping the v2 result in the paper also strengthens the baselines. The tuned phase+quality/risk threshold is not a straw baseline. It is the strongest old result, promoted into the full-scale benchmark. The boundary field has to beat it on safety, chatter, recovery, and utility under shift.

\section{Policy Failure Taxonomy}

Confidence-only switching fails by confusing intent clarity with physical admissibility. It gives authority to users who clearly intend an action even when contact risk or force margin says the robot should retain protection.

Timer-decay switching fails by using schedule as a proxy for state. It may be reasonable in scripted tasks, but it cannot tell whether the current phase is safe, whether the user has become noisy, or whether latency has changed.

The tuned quality/risk threshold fails by ignoring phase and hysteresis. It improves safety relative to confidence and timer rules but still behaves like a static surface in a moving state space.

The tuned phase+quality/risk threshold is much stronger. Its failure is not raw detection but stability under shifted boundaries. It has high F1, but it chatters and loses utility because a fixed phase gate and fixed thresholds cannot absorb all calibration and contact shifts.

Scalar risk-score arbitration fails by compression. It sees many variables, but it compresses them into one score and lacks explicit cede/reclaim asymmetry. The result is moderate utility with high chatter.

\clearpage
\section{Oracle Gap}

The oracle boundary supervisor reaches 0.738 utility, while the boundary field reaches 0.680. The gap is useful because it shows the benchmark is not saturated. Most of the gap comes from residual needless intervention, missed reclaim, and imperfect boundary F1. The oracle has privileged boundary supervision and lower residual risk floors.

Closing this gap would require either learning a richer boundary representation or gathering physical traces. A learned field could model nonlinear contact dynamics, user adaptation, and robot-specific authority cost. Hardware traces would reveal whether the simulated force, latency, and chatter proxies match real interaction outcomes. The current paper stops before those claims.

The field's advantage over tuned thresholds is still meaningful. It has roughly half the unsafe ceding, lower missed reclaim, and much lower chatter than the strongest threshold baseline. The oracle gap says there is more to learn; it does not erase the non-oracle improvement.

\section{Hardware Translation Plan}

A hardware study should preserve the same contrast. It should include a confidence-only policy, a timer policy, tuned quality/risk thresholds, tuned phase+quality/risk thresholds, scalar arbitration, a learned or hand-designed boundary field, and a calibrated oracle proxy. It should include tasks where ceding is usually good, tasks where reclaim is usually required, and mixed tasks where the correct authority mode changes.

The hardware setup should log synchronized user input, robot state, force or torque, contact phase, task phase, authority mode, and intervention events. It should include user calibration changes, contact dynamics changes, and sensing delays. It should measure not only task completion but unsafe ceding, missed reclaim, needless intervention, and chatter.

The strongest hardware test would be cross-condition transfer: tune thresholds on one user, robot, or contact regime, then evaluate on another. The central prediction is that static thresholds will remain competitive in-distribution but lose stability under shift, while a boundary field with explicit state variables and hysteresis will degrade more gracefully.

\section{Risk Controls}

A boundary field should not be deployed as a blind override. It should expose uncertainty. If force estimates are noisy, intent estimates are intermittent, or robot/user state is asynchronous, the field should widen the boundary and slow transfer. If the user is overconfident in a contact phase, the field should require stronger physical margin before ceding. If the robot is in a high-intrusion platform such as an exoskeleton, needless intervention should carry a higher cost.

The policy should also report why authority changed. Was authority ceded because phase was open, input quality was high, and margin was wide? Was authority reclaimed because contact risk rose, latency increased, or force margin shrank? This explanation is not cosmetic. It makes debugging possible when authority transfer fails.

Finally, the controller should include fallback modes. When the field is uncertain near a boundary, it can preserve shared control, slow down, request confirmation, or choose a safer trajectory. The field should improve arbitration, not remove ordinary safety layers.

\section{Final Claim Boundary}

The final claim is specific. Authority transfer should be modeled as a physical boundary field when safety and task value depend on phase, contact, user quality, risk, latency, and uncertainty. The evidence is a deterministic full-scale benchmark. It is not a hardware safety certificate, not a claim that thresholds are useless, and not a claim that the exact coefficients should be deployed.

The positive finding is that, under shifted authority boundaries, the field is the strongest non-oracle policy by aggregate utility and safety-stability tradeoff. The tuned phase threshold remains a serious competitor and slightly leads boundary F1. The field wins because it reduces unsafe ceding, missed reclaim, and chatter while preserving success. That is the contribution.

\clearpage
\section{Calibration Versus Generalization}

The old threshold result is best understood as calibration success. A tuned phase+quality/risk rule found the old decision surface because the train and holdout cases came from the same small family. That is not a defect in thresholding; it is a reminder that authority labels can be easy to fit when the boundary does not move. The v3 benchmark treats that success as a baseline rather than as a conclusion.

Generalization is harder because each factor can move the boundary in a different direction. User calibration shift changes the meaning of quality and confidence. Contact dynamics shift changes how much margin is needed before ceding. Sensing shift changes whether the state is current enough to trust. Robot platform shift changes the cost of unnecessary intervention and the quality of force control. Scenario shift changes whether human authority is naturally desirable or naturally risky.

The boundary field addresses this by composing variables that each have physical meaning. It does not only ask whether risk is below a threshold. It asks whether phase, user quality, margin, contact state, latency, uncertainty, and previous authority mode jointly permit transfer. This is why the field can be slightly behind the tuned threshold on raw F1 while still winning utility. It is less brittle near the boundary and less likely to produce rapid authority oscillations.

The practical lesson is that calibration should be treated as the first test, not the final test. A policy that performs perfectly on a narrow authority-label split should then be evaluated under boundary movement. If it remains safe, stable, and useful, it has evidence for shared-autonomy deployment. If it collapses, the calibration was only local.

\clearpage
\section{Boundary Field Feature Card}

The boundary field uses several feature groups. Task phase describes whether the current interaction is naturally human-led, robot-led, or shared. User input quality describes smoothness, consistency, and controllability of the human command. Intent confidence describes whether the system understands the user's intended direction, but it is not allowed to dominate the decision by itself. Contact risk describes force, collision, slip, payload, human proximity, and fragile-object hazards. Physical margin describes distance or force margin to the relevant limit. Latency and desynchronization describe whether the robot/user state estimate is current. Uncertainty describes sensing noise, dropout, and calibration shift. Previous authority mode supports hysteresis.

Each feature group has a failure it protects against. Phase protects against ceding during contact insertion simply because intent is clear. Quality protects against tremor and noisy joystick input. Risk and margin protect against physical harm. Latency protects against stale intent or force estimates. Uncertainty protects against overconfident switching when sensors degrade. Hysteresis protects against chatter.

The field is intentionally interpretable. A deployed version should be able to say, for example, that authority was retained because contact risk rose while margin narrowed, or that authority was ceded because phase was open, user quality was high, and uncertainty was low. This explanation is operationally useful. It tells the engineer whether a failure came from user modeling, force estimation, phase detection, or the hysteresis rule.

The benchmark coefficients are not sacred. A real robot would learn or tune them. What matters is the decomposition: authority transfer should expose its physical reasons instead of hiding them inside a scalar confidence score.

\clearpage
\section{Stress-Slice Interpretation}

The shift table in the main paper reports only the proposed boundary field. The full CSV contains every policy in every shift regime. The intended reading is comparative. In-distribution results test calibration. User calibration shift tests whether the policy over-trusts input quality or intent. Contact dynamics shift tests whether the policy over-trusts a fixed risk threshold. Compounded shift tests whether the policy can remain stable when several assumptions fail together.

The tuned phase+quality/risk threshold is strong because it has the right ingredients: phase, quality, and risk. Its weakness is rigidity. If the meaning of quality changes for a fatigued or tremor-heavy operator, the same threshold can cede too much or reclaim too often. If contact dynamics shift, the same risk threshold no longer maps to the same physical margin. The boundary field retains those ingredients but weights them through uncertainty and hysteresis, so it degrades more smoothly.

Scenario-level F1 should be interpreted with class balance in mind. Free-space alignment and remote inspection have fewer true reclaim boundaries, so F1 can be lower even when utility is high. Contact insertion, slip recovery, mobile docking, and peg-in-hole have frequent reclaim boundaries, so F1 is higher and directly reflects safety behavior. This is why the paper reports success, unsafe ceding, missed reclaim, chatter, and utility alongside F1.

The most important stress-slice pattern is not a single best number. It is the combination of low unsafe ceding, low missed reclaim, low chatter, and high success. Authority transfer is a control problem, not only a classifier.

\clearpage
\section{Deployment Protocol Sketch}

A practical system would run the boundary field as a supervisory layer around an existing shared-control policy. The base shared-control policy can still infer intent, predict goals, blend commands, or optimize robot assistance. The boundary field decides whether a proposed authority transfer is admissible.

The protocol has five steps. First, estimate task phase, user input quality, intent confidence, contact risk, physical margin, latency, uncertainty, and current authority mode. Second, compute candidate cede and reclaim scores. Third, apply hysteresis so small fluctuations near a boundary do not trigger rapid switching. Fourth, execute the authority decision with rate limits and safety monitors. Fifth, log the reason for the transfer or non-transfer.

The field should be conservative when uncertainty is high. If force sensing is noisy, if intent estimates are intermittent, or if robot and user state are asynchronous, the system should preserve shared control or slow the task until confidence improves. In high-intrusion systems such as exoskeletons, needless intervention should be penalized more strongly. In high-contact systems such as insertion or human-proximity manipulation, unsafe ceding should be penalized more strongly.

The protocol also suggests active recovery. If the field is uncertain, the robot can request a slower command, ask for confirmation, move to a safer intermediate pose, reduce speed, or gather more sensing. A boundary field should not be a one-shot switch. It should be a structured way to decide when authority transfer needs more evidence.
