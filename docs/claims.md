# Claims

## Core claims
1. Authority transfer in shared autonomy is better modeled as boundary inference in physical interaction space than as confidence-only arbitration.
2. Physical context can reverse the direction of optimal authority even when user intent remains stable.
3. A hysteretic boundary policy can reduce oscillatory switching and improve task success under phase changes and latency.

## Supported claims
- Many prior papers use intent, switch timing, or task context as the proxy for authority.
- These proxies leave hidden assumptions about phase invariance and monotone confidence-to-assistance mapping.

## Claims requiring evidence
- The boundary policy outperforms confidence-only baselines on the synthetic benchmark.
- The policy remains robust when user intent is correct but physical context makes human control temporarily worse.
- The policy is not equivalent to threshold tuning.

## Claims to avoid unless proven
- universal superiority on real robots
- a new theory of all shared autonomy
- zero-shot generalization beyond the tested contexts
